Solution 3.3.11.53.1

Generalizing our work in QuestionÂ 52, we find the approximations:

\begin{equation*} \int_a^{b+1}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x} \lt \sum_{n=a}^b \frac{1}{n} \lt \int_{a-1}^{b}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x} \end{equation*}

when \(a \ge 2\text{.}\) The inequality \(\int\limits_a^{b+1}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x}\lt\sum\limits_{n=a}^b \frac{1}{n}\) can be read off of the sketch

and the inequality \(\sum\limits_{n=a}^b \frac{1}{n}\lt\int\limits_{a-1}^{b}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x}\) can be read off of the sketch

We will evaluate the total population by writing

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{2 \times 10^6}\frac{2\times 10^6}{n} =\sum_{n=1}^{a-1}\frac{2\times 10^6}{n} +\sum_{n=a}^{2 \times 10^6}\frac{2\times 10^6}{n} \end{equation*}

and applying the above integral approximations to the second sum. We want our error to be less than one million, so we need to choose a value of \(a\) such that:

\begin{align*} \underbrace{2\times 10^6\int_{a-1}^{2\times 10^6} \frac{1}{x}\dee{x}}_{\text{upper bound}} - \underbrace{2\times 10^6\int_{a}^{2\times 10^6+1} \frac{1}{x}\dee{x}}_{\text{lower bound}} & \lt 10^6\\ \int_{a-1}^{2\times 10^6} \frac{1}{x}\dee{x} - \int_{a}^{2\times 10^6+1} \frac{1}{x}\dee{x} & \lt \frac{1}{2}\\ \left[\log\left(2\times 10^6 \right)-\log(a-1)\right]-\left[\log\left(2\times 10^6+1 \right)-\log(a)\right]& \lt \frac12\\ \left[\log\left(2\times 10^6 \right)-\log\left(2\times 10^6+1 \right)\right]+\left[ \log(a)-\log(a-1)\right]& \lt \frac12\\ \log\left(\frac{2\times 10^6}{2\times 10^6+1} \right)+ \log\left(\frac{a}{a-1} \right)& \lt \frac{1}{2} \end{align*}

The first term is extremely close to 0, so we ignore it.

\begin{align*} \log\left(\frac{a}{a-1} \right)& \lt \frac{1}{2}\\ \frac{a}{a-1}& \lt e^{1/2}=\sqrt{e}\\ a& \lt a\sqrt{e}-\sqrt{e}\\ \sqrt{e}& \lt a(\sqrt{e}-1)\\ \frac{\sqrt{e}}{\sqrt{e}-1}& \lt a \end{align*}

Since \(\dfrac{\sqrt{e}}{\sqrt{e}-1} \approx 2.5\text{,}\) we use \(a=3\text{.}\) That is, we will approximate the value of \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=3}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{1}{n}\) using an integral. Then, we will use that approximation to estimate our total population.

\begin{align*} \int_3^{2\times 10^6+1}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x}& \lt \sum_{n=3}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{1}{n} \lt \int_2^{2\times10^6}\frac{1}{x}\dee{x}\\ \log\left(2\times 10^6+1\right)-\log(3) & \lt \sum_{n=3}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{1}{n} \lt \log\left(2\times10^6\right)-\log(2)\\ \textcolor{red}{1+\frac12}+\log\left(2\times 10^6+1\right)-\log(3) & \lt \sum_{n=\textcolor{red}{1}}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{1}{n} \lt \textcolor{red}{1+\frac12}+\log\left(2\times10^6\right)\\ \amp\hskip2in-\log(2)\\ \frac32+ \log\left(\frac{2\times 10^6+1}{3}\right) & \lt \sum_{n=1}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{1}{n} \lt \frac32+6\log(10) \end{align*}

So the population, namely \(\sum_{n=1}^{2\times 10^6}\frac{\textcolor{red}{2\times10^6}}{n}\text{,}\) is between

\begin{equation*} \textcolor{red}{2\times10^6}\Big(\frac32+ \log\left(\tfrac{2}{3}\times 10^6+\tfrac{1}{3}\right)\Big)=29,820,091 \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \textcolor{red}{2\times10^6}\Big(\frac32+6\log\left(10\right)\Big)=30,631,021 \end{equation*}
