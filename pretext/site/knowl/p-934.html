  • In this example we should set \(u=3x\text{,}\) and substitute \(\dee{x} \rightarrow \frac{1}{u'(x)}\dee{u} = \frac{1}{3}\dee{u}.\) When we do this we also have to convert the limits of the integral: \(u(0)=0\) and \(u(\pi/2)=3\pi/2\text{.}\) This gives
    \begin{align*} \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\cos(3x)\dee{x} &= \int_0^{\frac{3\pi}{2}} \cos(u) \frac{1}{3}\dee{u}\\ &= \left[\frac{1}{3} \sin(u) \right]_0^{\frac{3\pi}{2}}\\ &= \frac{\sin(3\pi/2)-\sin(0)}{3}\\ &= \frac{-1-0}{3} = - \frac{1}{3}. \end{align*}
  • We can also do this example more directly using the above theorem. Since \(\sin(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(\cos(x)\text{,}\) TheoremÂ 1.4.10 tells us that \(\frac{\sin(3x)}{3}\) is an antiderivative of \(\cos(3x)\text{.}\) Hence
    \begin{align*} \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\cos(3x)\dee{x} &= \left[ \frac{\sin(3x)}{3} \right]_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\\ &= \frac{\sin(3\pi/2)-\sin(0)}{3}\\ &= -\frac{1}{3}. \end{align*}
