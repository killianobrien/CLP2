We will not fully prove that the intuitive definition (EZ) and the computational Definition B.2.1 are equivalent. But we will do so in the special case that \(z=iy\text{,}\) with \(y\) real. Under (EZ),

\begin{equation*} e^{iy} = 1 +iy +\frac{(iy)^2}{2!}+\frac{(iy)^3}{3!}+\frac{(iy)^4}{4!} +\frac{(iy)^5}{5!}+\frac{(iy)^6}{6!}+\cdots \end{equation*}

The even terms in this expansion are

\begin{equation*} 1 +\frac{(iy)^2}{2!}+\frac{(iy)^4}{4!}+\frac{(iy)^6}{6!}+\cdots =1 -\frac{y^2}{2!}+\frac{y^4}{4!}-\frac{y^6}{6!}+\cdots =\cos y \end{equation*}

and the odd terms in this expansion are

\begin{equation*} iy +\frac{(iy)^3}{3!}+\frac{(iy)^5}{5!}+\cdots =i\Big(y-\frac{y^3}{3!}+\frac{y^5}{5!}+\cdots\Big) =i\sin y \end{equation*}

Adding the even and odd terms together gives us that, under (EZ), \(e^{iy}\) is indeed equal to \(\cos y + i\sin y\text{.}\) 3  As a consequence, we have

\begin{equation*} e^{i\pi}=-1 \end{equation*}

which gives an amazing linking between calculus (\(e\)), geometry (\(\pi\)), algebra (\(i\)) and the basic number \(-1\text{.}\)
