The unknown function \(f(x)\) satisfies an equation that involves the derivative of \(f\text{.}\) That means we're in differential equation territory. Specifically, we are told that \(y=f(x)\) obeys the separable differential equation \(\diff{y}{x}=xy\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \diff{y}{x} &= xy\\ \frac{\dee{y}}{y} &= x\,\dee{x}\\ \int \frac{\dee{y}}{y} &= \int x\, \dee{x}\\ \log|y| &=\frac{x^2}{2} + C\\ \end{align*}

To determine \(C\) we set \(x=0\) and \(y=e\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \log e &=\frac{0^2}{2}+ C\\ 1&= C\\ \end{align*}

So, the solution is

\begin{align*} \log|y|&= \frac{x^2}{2} + 1\\ \end{align*}

We are told that \(y=f(x) \gt 0\text{,}\) so may drop the absolute value signs.

\begin{align*} \log y &= \frac{x^2}{2}+1\\ y&=e^{1+\frac{1}{2}x^2}=e\cdot e^{x^2/2} \end{align*}
