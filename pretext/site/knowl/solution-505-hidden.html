This is a separable differential equation that we solve in the usual way.

\begin{align*} \diff{y}{x} &= -xy^3\\ - \frac{\dee{y}}{y^3} &= x\dee{x}\\ \int -\frac{\dee{y}}{y^3} &= \int x\dee{x}\\ - \frac{y^{-2}}{-2} &= \frac{x^2}{2}+C\\ y^{-2}&=x^2+2C. \tag{$*$}\\ \end{align*}

To have \(y=-\frac{1}{4}\) when \(x=0\text{,}\) we must choose \(C\) to obey

\begin{align*} {\Big(-\frac{1}{4} \Big)}^{-2}& =0 +2C\\ 16&= 2C\\ \end{align*}

So, from (\(*\)),

\begin{align*} y^{-2}&=x^2+2C =x^2+16\\ y^2&=\frac{1}{x^2+16}\\ \end{align*}

Now, we have two potential candidates for \(y(x)\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} y&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+16}}\qquad\text{OR}\qquad y=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+16}}\\ \end{align*}

We know \(y=-\frac14\) when \(x=0\text{.}\) The only function above that fits this is

\begin{align*} y&=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+16}} \end{align*}

So, \(f(x) = -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+16}}\text{.}\)
