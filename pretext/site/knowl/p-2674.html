  1. If the integral \(\int_a^R f(x)\dee{x}\) exists for all \(R \gt a\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^\infty f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\int_a^R f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when the limit exists (and is finite).
  2. If the integral \(\int_r^b f(x)\dee{x}\) exists for all \(r \lt b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^b f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{r\rightarrow-\infty}\int_r^b f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when the limit exists (and is finite).
  3. If the integral \(\int_r^R f(x)\dee{x}\) exists for all \(r \lt R\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{r\rightarrow-\infty}\int_r^c f(x)\dee{x} +\lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\int_c^R f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when both limits exist (and are finite). Any \(c\) can be used.

When the limit(s) exist, the integral is said to be convergent. Otherwise it is said to be divergent.
