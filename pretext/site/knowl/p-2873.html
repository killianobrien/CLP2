There are two sources of error: the upper bound is \(t\text{,}\) rather than infinity, and we're using an approximation with some finite number of intervals, \(n\text{.}\) Our plan is to first find a value of \(t\) that introduces an error of no more than \(\frac{1}{2}10^{-4}\text{.}\) That is, we'll find a value of \(t\) such that \(\int_t^\infty \frac{e^{-x}}{1+x} \dee{x} \leq \frac{1}{2}10^{-4}\text{.}\) After that, we'll find a value of \(n\) that approximates \(\int_0^t \frac{e^{-x}}{x+1} \dee{x}\) to within \(\frac{1}{2}10^{-4}\text{.}\) Then, all together, our error will be at most \(\frac{1}{2}10^{-4} + \frac{1}{2}10^{-4} = 10^{-4}\text{,}\) as desired.
