Notice that (c) is used when the integrand is unbounded at some point in the middle of the domain of integration, such as was the case in our original example

\begin{gather*} \int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{x^2} \dee{x} \end{gather*}

A quick computation shows that this integral diverges to \(+\infty\)

\begin{align*} \int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{x^2} \dee{x} &= \lim_{a \to 0^-} \int_{-1}^a \frac{1}{x^2} \dee{x} + \lim_{b \to 0^+} \int_b^1 \frac{1}{x^2}\dee{x}\\ &= \lim_{a \to 0^-} \left[1-\frac{1}{a} \right] + \lim_{b \to 0^+} \left[\frac{1}{b}-1 \right]\\ &= + \infty \end{align*}
