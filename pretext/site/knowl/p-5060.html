Usually with factorials, we want to use the divergence test or the ratio test. Since the terms are indeed tending towards zero, we are left with the ratio test.

\begin{align*} \amp\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=\frac{\frac{(n+1)!(n+1)!}{(2n+2)!}}{\frac{n!n!}{(2n)!}}=\frac{(n+1)!(n+1)!}{n!n!}\cdot\frac{(2n)!}{(2n+2)!}\\ &=\tfrac{\textcolor{red}{(n+1)}{(n)}{(n-1)}\cdots{(1)}}{{n}{(n-1)}\cdots{(1)}} \cdot\tfrac{\textcolor{red}{(n+1)}{(n)}{(n-1)}\cdots{(1)}}{{n}{(n-1)}\cdots{(1)}} \cdot \tfrac{{(2n)}{(2n-1)}{(2n-2)}\cdots{(1)}}{\textcolor{red}{(2n+2)(2n+1)}{(2n)}{(2n-1)}{(2n-2)}\cdots{(1)}}\\ &=(n+1)(n+1) \cdot \frac{1}{(2n+2)(2n+1)} \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}&=\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}\frac{(n+1)(n+1)}{(2n+2)(2n+1)} =\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}\frac{(n+1)(n+1)}{2(n+1)(2n+1)}\\ \amp=\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}\frac{n+1}{4n+2}=\frac{1}{4} \end{align*}

Since the limit is a number less than 1, the series converges by the ratio test.
